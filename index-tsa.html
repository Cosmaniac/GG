About TSA

Standards

  • TSA Motto - “Learning to Lead in a Technical World”
  • TSA Creed - "I believe that Technology Education holds an important place in my life in the technical world. I believe there is a need for the development of good attitudes concerning work, tools, materials, experimentation, and processes of industry. Guided by my teachers, artisans from industry, and my own initiative, I will strive to do my best in making my school, community, state, and nation better places in which to live. I will accept the responsibilities that are mine. I will accept the theories that are supported by proper evidence. I will explore on my own for safer, more effective methods of working and living. I will strive to develop a cooperative attitude and will exercise tact and respect for other individuals. Through the work of my hands and mind, I will express my ideas to the best of my ability. I will make it my goal to do better each day the task before me, and to be steadfast in my belief in my God, and my fellow Americans."
  • TSA Mission Statement - The Technology Student Association enhances personal development, leadership, and career opportunities in STEM, whereby members apply and integrate these concepts through intracurricular activities, competitions, and related programs.

History

  • Created in 1978, TSA is a student organization designed to create members with an extensive knowledge of STEM fields and leadership skills. With 250,000 members, TSA is among the largest student organizations in the world.
  • TSA History - Originally a part of AIASA, (American Industrial Arts Student Association), TSA became focused on mainstream technology, eventually evolving into fields such as robotics, programming, and of course, website design. TSA has also broadened its horizons to other causes, such as the American Cancer Society, for which TSA raised money to fund research and experimentation.

CTE Interview with Mr. Kapptie

  • Q: What classes do you teach at Skyline?
    A: Exploring Computer Science 1, Exploring Computer Science 2 (Robotics), Digital Media 1 and 2, Computer Programming 1 and 2, IB Computer Programming, Desktop Publishing 1 and 2 (Yearbook)
  • Q: How long have you been teaching CTE classes?
    A: I have been teaching CTE courses for 10 years at four schools.
  • Q: What is your favorite part about teaching these CTE classes?
    A: I love the nature of technology courses because they always change and change is something I thrive on, and honestly, I learn more from the students than they [learn from] me.

  • Q: Why is it important for schools to have CTE courses?
    A: I hear from parents all the time “why does my daughter/son need a technology course when they were born with it and immersed in it?” I find very quickly that students really don’t understand the digital paradigm at the most fundamental level. CTE courses bring into focus just how technology works and what constitutes an ethical approach to this information age we currently live in.
  • Q: How will the skills your students learn now help them in the future?
    A: Hopefully these skills will be used to make individuals more productive. My goal is to teach how technology can make a student more creative, more analytical, and more versatile, and never replace people, but empower them.

Visual

Skyline offers a several intensive Visual courses for our students.

  • Digital Media I: Students learn to express their creativity through photo manipulation, graphic design, web design, animation, digital music composing, and video production. Students can choose to participate in an optional film festival. At the end of the class, they will have a portfolio that showcases what they have done throughout the year.
  • Digital Media II: Students will expand on the knowledge they gained in Digital Media I by further exploring 3-D graphics, animation, scripting, storyboarding, video production, audio mixing, and project development. This class is designed to help students develop marketable skills in multimedia production.
  • Graphics: This course educates students to run applications like Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop, InDesign, and Photobooth. This software is used to create cool designs for a variety of products like T-shirts, vinyl stickers, notepads, magnets, buttons, and other products. Students get an exciting, hands-on, real world experience in digital media technology that gives them that competitive edge for post-high school education and the future job market.
  • Advanced Graphics Communications (Graphics 2): This second year course takes students into the advanced levels of Adobe Illustrator, Photoshop, and InDesign to produce exciting, professional-quality designs and products. Students work with school groups and other organizations to develop customer products and understand professional relationships, work flow, and management and quality skills so they are prepared to meet the demands of college and careers. Students learn about business and money to help give students a competitive edge.

Consumer Sciences

Skyline’s CTE program not only gives students a technical background but also skills that will assist them with their day-to-day lives.

  • Furniture Design: Design and build a personal heirloom-quality piece of furniture or select a project from a list of pre-planned projects, including cedar chests, entertainment centers, long boards, bed frames, grandfather clocks, dressers, and more. Learn how to safely use a variety of tools and machinery with accuracy and efficiency. Learn how to operate computer-controlled machinery.
  • Woodworking I (Chest): Designing, cutting, fitting, assembling, and finishing are some of the many skills students will learn. In this introductory course, they will have the opportunity to build a personal, heirloom quality project, plus a group-manufactured project.
  • Woodworking II (Electric Guitar): This class has an emphasis on computer-controlled cutting. In this course, students will build an electric guitar, as well as a group-manufactured project.

Computer Sciences

Computer sciences are a core part of Skyline’s CTE program and offer wide-ranging opportunities in programming languages and IDEs.

  • Computer Science Principles: This course seeks to broaden participation in computer science by emphasizing the principles of computer science, rather than just the programming aspect.
  • Computer Programming I: Students will be introduced to the fundamentals of computer programming and software engineering, as well as their applications. They will learn to design, code, and test their own programs in languages such as C++, C#, Java, Python, and Processing, applying mathematical concepts along the way.
  • Computer Programming II: This second-year course is meant for students pursuing IB or AP computer science. Students continue to study languages like C++, C#, Java, and Python in depth as preparation for the state, AP, or IB exam.

Robotics

Skyline High School’s robotics team is a powerhouse in statewide competitions and is supplemented by the assortment of computer science and robotics courses offered in our CTE program.

  • Exploring Computer Science: This course introduces students to the breadth of the field of computer science through an exploration of engaging and accessible topics. The course is designed to focus the conceptual ideas of computing and help students understand why certain tools or languages might be utilized to solve particular problems. The goal of the course is to develop in students the computational thinking practices of algorithm development, as well as problem solving and programming skills, within the context of issues that are relevant to students. Students will also be introduced to topics such as interface design, limits of computers, and societal and ethical issues.
  • Exploring Computer Science II (Robotics for Competition): This course follows the standards and objectives for ECS II with a special focus on preparing robots for various competitions around Utah. Students will engage in problem solving, human computer interaction, data analysis, programming, and basic engineering concepts specific to modern robotics. A web-based portfolio of your strategies and experiences will be prepared at the conclusion of the course.

Auto/Shop

Many hands-on courses like Woodshop and Auto Mechanics are also available as a part of Skyline’s CTE program.

  • Intro/Beginning Auto Mechanics: Learn to use and operate the same tools and equipment that today’s master automotive technicians use. Explore, diagnose, and repair various computer- controlled systems. Prepare for advanced professional certification, professional employment, and further skill development.
  • General Service Technician (Auto Mechanics II): This class is for students with a good, solid knowledge of cars, their systems (brakes, suspension, engines, etc.), and hand tools. This course builds upon the basic automotive knowledge students have about cars. Over 60% of the work is in the shop repairing live vehicles, such as students’ personal vehicles, building shop project cars, and other fun-filled projects.
  • General Service Technician II: This course allows students to continue their automotive experience. This course works well for those interested in the technical or engineering programs of various colleges. The majority of class time is spent in the shop repairing and maintaining motorized vehicles. The class will also dive into the racing and car show sides of the automotive field.

Trade Studies

Many Skyline CTE classes also familiarize students with specific trades, such as law enforcement and sports medicine.

  • Law Enforcement: This is an introductory course for those who want to pursue a career in law enforcement. This course, taught by our school officer, includes courtroom and trial procedures, important court cases, rights of the accused, and problems relating to law enforcement. There is also a field trip to the jail and the prison each semester.
  • Internship Experience: This course is designed to give students valuable workplace experience in a career of their choice. Students are placed in a business setting, where they receive on-the-job mentoring. Interns spend 60 hours at the worksite and 16 hours in the classroom. Students receive classroom instruction on workplace expectations, resume writing, applications, interviewing, harassment, labor and safety laws, interpersonal communication, and creating a portfolio.
  • Sports Medicine: Learn about human anatomy and how to prevent, evaluate, and treat injuries. Master first aid techniques, and discover how patients are rehabilitated. This class gives you a foundation for careers in physical therapy and athletic training.
  • Yearbook (Desktop Publishing): The students in this course plan and produce the school yearbook, The Aquilian. Students receive training in layout, design, photography, cropping and editing photos, and writing stories and captions.
